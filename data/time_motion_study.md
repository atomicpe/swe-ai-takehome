# Time and Motion Study: Riverside Manufacturing — Assembly & Packing Floor

**Study Date:** March 14-15, 2026
**Observer:** J. Whitfield, Industrial Engineering Consultant
**Facility:** Riverside Manufacturing, Plant 3 — Small Electronics Assembly
**Shift Observed:** Day shift (7:00 AM — 3:30 PM)

---

## Day 1 Observations

### Morning — Assembly Line Alpha

**7:02 AM** — Operator A (Maria Chen) arrives at Station 1 (PCB soldering). She spends approximately 4 minutes retrieving her tools from the shared locker area, which is located at the far end of the floor, roughly 45 meters from her station. She begins soldering at 7:06 AM.

**7:06 AM - 7:52 AM** — Maria works through a batch of 24 PCBs. Average time per board is approximately 1.9 minutes. She completes the batch and places them in the transfer bin for Station 2.

**7:15 AM** — Operator B (David Park) arrives at Station 2 (component mounting). He has no work yet because Station 1's first batch hasn't arrived. He waits idle at his station, checking his phone. He doesn't begin work until 7:53 AM when Maria's first batch arrives.

**7:53 AM - 8:41 AM** — David mounts components on the 24 boards. His overall average is 2.0 minutes per board including a 6-minute stoppage at 8:12 AM to recalibrate his placement tool after noticing alignment drift. His actual soldering pace when working is faster, but the mid-batch recalibration brings the average up.

**8:00 AM** — Quality Inspector (Tomoko Sato) arrives and begins her first walk-through of the line. She spends 3 minutes at Station 1 reviewing Maria's solder quality on the remaining boards. She finds no issues and moves to Station 2.

**8:05 AM - 8:14 AM** — Tomoko inspects 8 of David's completed boards. She flags 2 boards with marginal component alignment and sets them aside in the rework bin. This takes her 9 minutes total.

**8:15 AM** — Material Handler (James Okonkwo) brings a new tray of raw PCBs to Station 1. He makes the trip from the stockroom (about 60 meters away) carrying one tray of 24 boards. The round trip takes him 8 minutes including loading time. Maria doesn't need these yet — she still has 12 boards from her initial stock.

**8:30 AM** — James makes another trip to bring solder wire to Station 1. Maria mentioned she was running low during his previous delivery, but he had to go back because he could only carry one item at a time. This second trip takes 7 minutes.

**8:45 AM** — Operator C (Priya Sharma) at Station 3 (testing) has been idle since 7:00 AM, waiting for completed boards from Station 2. She receives her first batch of 22 boards (24 minus 2 rework) at 8:42 AM. She begins functional testing.

**8:42 AM - 9:37 AM** — Priya tests boards at approximately 2.5 minutes each. She identifies 1 board that fails the voltage test and places it in the reject bin. She completes 22 boards (21 pass, 1 fail) and has no more work until David's next batch arrives.

**9:00 AM** — Tomoko begins her second inspection round. She returns to Station 1 and spends 4 minutes reviewing Maria's work. She then walks to Station 3 and spends 12 minutes re-testing 3 of Priya's passed boards as a spot check. All 3 pass.

### Mid-Morning — Packing Area

**9:15 AM** — Packer (Luis Fernandez) at the packing station receives the first 21 tested boards. He inspects each visually (about 30 seconds per board), places them in anti-static bags, boxes them in sets of 6, and labels each box. His total time for 21 boards is 35 minutes, finishing at 9:50 AM. He notes that the label printer jammed twice, costing approximately 4 minutes total.

**9:50 AM - 10:15 AM** — Luis has no boards to pack. He reorganizes his station and restocks packing materials, which takes about 10 minutes. He is idle for the remaining 15 minutes.

**10:00 AM** — James makes a trip to the packing area to deliver shipping labels. He discovers that the labels he brought are for the wrong product SKU. He returns to the stockroom and comes back with the correct labels at 10:18 AM. Total wasted time: 18 minutes.

### Late Morning — Assembly Line Alpha (continued)

**10:05 AM** — Maria completes her second batch of 24 boards at Station 1. She has been working steadily since 7:52 AM with a 15-minute break at 9:15 AM. Effective working time on this batch: approximately 1 hour 58 minutes for 24 boards, or about 4.9 minutes per board — notably slower than her morning pace of 1.9 minutes per board.

**10:06 AM** — David immediately begins his second batch. He's now caught up since Maria's first batch is done.

**10:30 AM** — Tomoko flags that Maria's solder quality has degraded on the second batch. She pulls 4 boards for rework. She spends 15 minutes discussing the issue with Maria, who says the new solder wire James delivered at 8:30 AM is a different alloy than what she normally uses.

**11:00 AM** — James is asked to bring the correct solder wire. He takes 12 minutes for the round trip. Maria has been idle since 10:45 AM waiting for the correct materials.

### Afternoon — Assembly Line Alpha

**11:12 AM** — Maria resumes soldering with the correct wire. She completes the remaining 8 boards from her second batch by 11:28 AM (2.0 min/board — back to normal pace).

**11:30 AM - 12:00 PM** — Lunch break for all operators. Line is idle.

**12:00 PM** — Maria begins her third batch. David is still working through his second batch (started at 10:06 AM, delayed by upstream quality issues). Priya is idle again, waiting.

**12:45 PM** — Tomoko returns from lunch and immediately pulls David aside. She has noticed that the 2 boards she flagged for rework at 8:05 AM are still sitting in the rework bin — nobody has re-worked them. David says rework isn't his responsibility; Maria says she wasn't told about them. The boards have been sitting for nearly 5 hours. Tomoko spends 20 minutes re-working them herself, even though rework is not formally part of her quality inspector role.

**12:50 PM** — Maria finishes her third batch (24 boards). Her pace is 1.9 min/board, consistent with her corrected-materials rate, with a few minutes lost to the rework discussion. Total boards soldered today: 72 (3 batches of 24).

**1:15 PM** — Shift Supervisor (Karen Walsh) walks the floor for her afternoon check. She spends approximately 25 minutes observing but doesn't intervene in any activities. She speaks briefly with each operator, asking about throughput. She does not notice the rework backlog issue because Tomoko has already cleared it.

**2:00 PM - 2:45 PM** — David is working on his third batch. He's now keeping pace with Maria's output. Priya is testing David's second batch, which arrived late due to the cascading delays.

**2:50 PM** — James begins end-of-day stockroom reconciliation. He counts remaining inventory at each station and notes discrepancies: Station 1 has 8 more PCBs than expected (the boards Maria didn't need from the 8:15 AM delivery that were never returned to stock). He spends 25 minutes on reconciliation.

**3:15 PM** — Line winds down. David has 6 boards remaining in his third batch. They will carry over to tomorrow.

## Day 1 — Warehouse & Shipping Dock

### Morning Receiving

**7:00 AM** — Receiving Clerk (Aisha Patel) opens the dock. Two deliveries are scheduled: raw materials from supplier Tanaka Components (expected 7:30 AM) and packaging supplies from BoxCo (expected 8:00 AM).

**7:45 AM** — Tanaka delivery arrives 15 minutes late. Aisha begins unloading with Forklift Operator (Robert "Bobby" Kim). The shipment contains 12 pallets. Bobby moves pallets from the truck to the receiving area while Aisha counts and inspects. They work in parallel — Bobby averages 4 minutes per pallet (load, drive, place, return), while Aisha takes about 3 minutes per pallet to count and log.

**7:45 AM - 8:33 AM** — Bobby completes all 12 pallets. However, Aisha is only on pallet 9 when Bobby finishes because she had to stop twice to answer phone calls from the front office (totaling about 7 minutes). Bobby waits idle for her to catch up on the last 3 pallets. She finishes at 8:42 AM.

**8:00 AM** — The BoxCo delivery arrives on time but cannot begin unloading because the Tanaka truck is still at the dock. There's only one dock bay. The BoxCo driver waits in the yard.

**8:50 AM** — Tanaka truck departs. Bobby backs the BoxCo truck into the dock. Unloading begins at 8:55 AM. This delivery is only 4 pallets — Bobby and Aisha complete it by 9:12 AM.

**Note:** The BoxCo truck waited approximately 50 minutes. The driver mentioned their company charges detention fees after 30 minutes.

### Inventory Putaway

**9:15 AM** — Bobby begins moving the received pallets from the receiving area to warehouse shelves. He has 16 pallets total (12 Tanaka + 4 BoxCo). His route takes him through a narrow aisle that requires him to slow down significantly. Average putaway time is 6 minutes per pallet.

**9:15 AM - 10:00 AM** — Bobby puts away 7 pallets. He then takes a break.

**10:15 AM** — Bobby resumes. Aisha has left the warehouse floor to process receiving paperwork in the office. Bobby now has no spotter for the high-shelf placements. Company policy requires a spotter for any placement above 3 meters. 5 of the remaining 9 pallets are designated for high shelves.

**10:15 AM - 10:39 AM** — Bobby puts away the 4 pallets that go on lower shelves (no spotter needed), averaging 6 minutes each. He then waits for Aisha to return.

**11:05 AM** — Aisha returns from the office. Bobby resumes high-shelf putaway with Aisha spotting. However, Aisha is also trying to update the inventory management system on her tablet between placements. This dual-tasking causes Bobby to wait an extra ~2 minutes per pallet while she finishes data entry before getting into spotting position. The 5 high-shelf pallets take 40 minutes total (8 min/pallet vs. the 6 min/pallet baseline).

**11:45 AM** — All putaway complete. Bobby has moved 16 pallets in approximately 1 hour 46 minutes of active work, but the wall-clock time from first pallet to last was 2 hours 30 minutes — the difference is breaks and waiting for Aisha.

### Afternoon — Shipping

**12:30 PM** — After lunch, Shipping Clerk (Nadia Volkov) begins processing outbound orders. She has 8 orders to ship today. She picks items from warehouse shelves using a paper pick list, stages them at the packing bench, and prepares shipping labels.

**12:30 PM - 1:15 PM** — Nadia picks items for orders 1-4. Average pick time is 11 minutes per order. However, on order 3, she cannot find the item in the location listed on her pick list. She spends an extra 9 minutes searching before locating it one aisle over — it was put away in the wrong location. (Note: Bobby was doing putaway this morning without Aisha verifying locations for the lower-shelf items.)

**1:15 PM - 1:45 PM** — Nadia packs and labels orders 1-4. Average pack time is 7 minutes per order. She notices order 2 has a special handling instruction ("fragile — double box") that wasn't flagged on the pick list. She has to unpack and re-pack it, adding 8 minutes.

**1:50 PM** — Nadia begins picking orders 5-8. Order 6 requires an item that is showing as in-stock in the system but the shelf is empty. Aisha's inventory update from this morning hasn't propagated yet — the Tanaka shipment items are received but the system still shows old quantities. Nadia has to walk to the receiving area to verify the item is physically present, find it on a pallet that hasn't been shelved yet (it was on a high shelf pallet that Bobby put away late), and bring it back. This detour takes 22 minutes.

**2:30 PM** — Nadia completes picking for orders 5-8 and begins packing. She finishes all 8 orders by 3:10 PM.

**3:10 PM** — Bobby loads the 8 outbound orders onto the shipping truck. He discovers that 2 orders are going to the same destination but were packed in separate boxes. He mentions to Nadia that combining them would save shipping costs, but she says the orders came in separately and she follows the pick list as written. The orders ship separately.

## Day 2 Observations

### Morning — Assembly Line Alpha

**7:00 AM** — All operators arrive on time. Maria has the correct solder wire from the start. James pre-staged materials at each station before the shift, arriving at 6:45 AM. This eliminated the morning tool retrieval trip (saving ~4 minutes) and the first material delivery trip.

**7:00 AM - 7:44 AM** — Maria completes a batch of 24 boards at 1.85 minutes per board. Consistent with Day 1 morning pace.

**7:02 AM** — David reviews his calibration tool before starting rather than discovering drift mid-batch. This takes 3 minutes but prevents the mid-batch stoppage observed on Day 1.

**7:44 AM** — David begins immediately. The Station 1-to-2 handoff gap is reduced from 38 minutes (Day 1) to 0 minutes because he started calibration review early.

**7:44 AM - 8:32 AM** — David completes the batch in 48 minutes (2.0 min/board). No mid-batch stoppages.

**8:00 AM** — Tomoko begins inspection. She finds 0 defects in Maria's batch (correct solder wire) and 1 marginal alignment in David's work. Only 1 board pulled for rework.

**8:33 AM** — Priya receives 23 boards and begins testing immediately. No idle gap (Day 1 gap was 1 hour 42 minutes).

**8:33 AM - 9:31 AM** — Priya tests all 23 boards. All pass. Her throughput is the same as Day 1 (2.5 min/board).

**9:30 AM** — Luis begins packing. He replaced the label printer ribbon before the shift started. No jams. He completes 23 boards in 33 minutes (down from 35 for 21 boards on Day 1 — the printer fix saved time even with 2 more boards).

### Morning — Warehouse Receiving (Day 2)

**7:00 AM** — Aisha arrives and pre-prints receiving documentation for today's expected deliveries. Bobby checks the forklift and stages empty pallets at the dock.

**7:30 AM** — Single delivery today: Tanaka Components, 8 pallets. Arrives on time. Bobby and Aisha work in sync — no phone interruptions. Bobby unloads while Aisha counts. All 8 pallets unloaded and verified by 8:05 AM (35 minutes vs. 57 minutes for 12 pallets on Day 1, adjusting for volume that's proportionally faster).

**8:10 AM** — Bobby begins putaway. Aisha accompanies him from the start, handling both spotting and real-time inventory updates. She updates the system as each pallet is placed rather than batching it for later. All 8 pallets put away by 8:58 AM (6 min/pallet, consistent baseline — no dual-tasking delays).

**9:00 AM** — Both Bobby and Aisha are free. On Day 1 at this time, they were still unloading the BoxCo truck and putaway hadn't started.

### Mid-Morning — A Role Confusion Incident (Day 2)

**9:30 AM** — The assembly floor's material handler James is on break. Priya at Station 3 runs out of test leads and needs replacements from the stockroom. Rather than wait for James, she walks to the stockroom herself (5 minute round trip). While she is away, 6 completed boards arrive from Station 2. They sit in the transfer bin untouched for 5 minutes.

**9:35 AM** — Bobby, who has finished his warehouse work, notices the idle boards at Station 3 while walking past the assembly floor on his way to the break room. He is not trained on the testing equipment but begins visually sorting the boards by markings, something he's seen Priya do. When Priya returns at 9:40 AM, she discovers Bobby has sorted 3 boards into the wrong priority order based on a marking convention he didn't understand. She has to re-sort them (2 minutes) and re-verify that none were placed in the wrong bin (3 minutes). Bobby's well-intentioned help cost 5 minutes net.

**10:15 AM** — Tomoko is conducting her inspection round on the assembly floor. Karen Walsh asks Tomoko to step away and help with an urgent customer complaint — a shipped board was found defective and the customer wants to know the inspection history. Tomoko spends 35 minutes pulling records in the office. During this time, Maria completes a batch that goes un-inspected. David processes it downstream without the usual quality gate. By the time Tomoko returns at 10:50 AM, 18 boards have passed through without inspection.

**10:50 AM - 11:10 AM** — Tomoko retroactively inspects the 18 boards that bypassed quality. She finds 1 defect. But she notes she cannot confirm these boards weren't mixed with previously inspected boards during the gap — the transfer bin doesn't separate inspected from uninspected units. She flags this as a process gap.

### Afternoon — Shipping (Day 2)

**12:30 PM** — Nadia begins afternoon shipping. She has 6 orders today. She modified her workflow based on yesterday's issues: she walks the pick locations first to verify stock before starting picks, and she reads special handling instructions at the start of each order rather than discovering them during packing.

**12:30 PM - 1:00 PM** — Pre-walk verification takes 15 minutes but she identifies one stock discrepancy upfront (avoids a mid-pick detour like Day 1's 22-minute incident). She has Aisha correct the inventory record before picking begins.

**1:00 PM - 2:15 PM** — Nadia picks and packs all 6 orders. Average total time per order is 12 minutes (vs. ~16 minutes on Day 1). No re-work, no searching, no mid-pick surprises.

**2:15 PM** — Nadia notices two orders are going to the same customer at different addresses. Unlike Day 1's same-destination issue, these genuinely need to ship separately. She documents this to distinguish it from the Day 1 case.

**2:30 PM** — Bobby loads outbound orders. All shipments out by 2:45 PM (vs. 3:10 PM on Day 1 with 2 more orders).

## Day 2 — Efficiency Comparison Summary

**Assembly Line Alpha:**
- By 10:03 AM on Day 2, the line has produced 23 packed, shipped-ready boards. On Day 1, the same milestone (first shipment-ready batch) wasn't reached until 9:50 AM, and only contained 21 boards.
- James's movement on Day 2: 2 round trips to stockroom by 10:00 AM vs. 4 trips on Day 1 by the same time. Pre-staging eliminated 2 trips. No wrong-item deliveries.
- Maria's pace remained at ~1.9 min/board throughout Day 2. The degradation seen on Day 1 (4.9 min/board on second batch) was attributable to the wrong solder wire, not fatigue.

**Warehouse & Shipping:**
- Receiving and putaway completed by 9:00 AM on Day 2 (8 pallets) vs. 11:45 AM on Day 1 (16 pallets). Even accounting for lower volume, the per-pallet time improved.
- Shipping throughput improved from ~16 min/order (Day 1) to ~12 min/order (Day 2) primarily from Nadia's pre-walk verification eliminating mid-pick disruptions.
- Zero inventory discrepancies encountered during picking on Day 2 (vs. 2 on Day 1), attributable to Aisha's real-time system updates during putaway.

## Observer Notes

### Assembly Floor
- The shared tool locker is a recurring source of delays. Multiple operators mentioned it's inconvenient. Personal tool storage at stations would save an estimated 5-8 minutes per operator per day.
- James's single-item carrying constraint caused multiple redundant trips. A cart or multi-bin carrier could reduce his trips by roughly 40%.
- Station 2's calibration drift was a known issue that David had reported previously. Preventive calibration before shift start (as done on Day 2) is clearly effective.
- Priya at Station 3 experienced the longest idle time on Day 1 (1 hour 42 minutes) due to upstream delays cascading. She cannot begin work until Station 2 completes, creating a hard dependency.
- The label printer issue at the packing station is a recurring maintenance item according to Luis.
- The rework process has no clear owner. Boards sat in the rework bin for nearly 5 hours on Day 1 because responsibility was ambiguous between Maria and David. Tomoko eventually did the rework herself, outside her defined role.
- The quality inspection gap on Day 2 (Tomoko pulled away for 35 minutes) exposed a process fragility: there is no backup inspector and no mechanism to hold uninspected boards separate from inspected ones.

### Warehouse & Shipping
- The single dock bay is a bottleneck. The BoxCo truck waited 50 minutes on Day 1, likely incurring detention fees. If multiple deliveries are common, a second bay or scheduled delivery windows would help.
- Bobby's putaway without Aisha verifying locations led to a mis-shelved item that cost Nadia 9 extra minutes during picking. The spotter policy exists for safety but also serves as an implicit accuracy check.
- Aisha's practice of batching inventory updates (Day 1) vs. real-time updates (Day 2) had a direct downstream impact on Nadia's picking accuracy. The Day 2 approach eliminated phantom stock issues entirely.
- Nadia's pre-walk verification on Day 2 was self-initiated, not a standard procedure. It was effective and should be considered for formalization.
- The same-destination shipping consolidation question (Day 1) reveals a gap between order-level processing and shipment-level optimization. The current process doesn't surface consolidation opportunities.

### Cross-Functional Observations
- Workers stepping outside their defined roles produced mixed results. Tomoko doing rework (positive — cleared a 5-hour backlog), Bobby sorting boards (negative — created re-work for Priya), Priya fetching her own materials (neutral — brief absence but boards accumulated). A naive classification of these as "helping" or "waste" would miss the nuance that the outcome depends on whether the person understands the task they're stepping into.
- Several efficiency gains on Day 2 came from workers preparing before their shift officially started (James at 6:45 AM, Bobby pre-checking the forklift, Luis replacing the printer ribbon). This raises a question about whether shift start times should be adjusted or whether pre-shift prep should be compensated separately.
- The observer notes that Maria's throughput drop on Day 1 second batch (4.9 min/board vs. 1.9 min/board) could easily be misattributed to fatigue, which is a common assumption in time studies. The actual root cause was wrong materials. Any automated analysis should be cautious about inferring causation from temporal patterns alone.
